# Literature Review

## Papers

### Colmer, Martin, Muuls & Wagner (2024) — "Does Pricing Carbon Mitigate Climate Change?"

**Key idea:** EU ETS induced French manufacturing firms to reduce CO2 emissions by 14-16% with no detectable economic contraction. Firms invested in cleaner production technology (technology switching) rather than outsourcing emissions. No evidence of carbon leakage.

**Relevance to this project:** Most directly comparable — uses French firm-level data, EU ETS, matched DiD. Their finding of no leakage at the firm level contrasts with Coster et al.'s finding of leakage through supply chain sourcing. Our network approach can study how policy propagates beyond directly regulated firms.

### Martinsson, Sajtos, Stromberg & Thomann (2024) — "The Effect of Carbon Pricing on Firm Emissions: Evidence from the Swedish CO2 Tax"

**Key idea:** Estimates emission-to-pricing elasticity of ~2 using 26 years of Swedish firm-level data. Substantial heterogeneity: highest emitters have lowest elasticities (~1.3). Financial constraints reduce elasticities in high-abatement-cost sectors. Carbon pricing accounts for ~30% higher counterfactual emissions.

**Relevance to this project:** Demonstrates that carbon pricing elasticities vary enormously across firms — relevant for understanding heterogeneous responses in production networks. Financial constraints finding connects to how firm characteristics mediate policy propagation.

**Notes/thoughts:** [Full notes](articles/notes_martinsson_et_al_2024.md)

### Coster, di Giovanni & Mejean (2025) — "Firms' Supply Chain Adaptation to Carbon Taxes"

**Key idea:** French firms shifted sourcing of ETS-regulated inputs toward non-EU countries as ETS became more stringent (4.3pp increase in non-EU regulated import share). Structural model shows CBAM is 7x more effective than ETS alone at reducing emissions, but at higher price cost. Leakage driven by downstream (non-ETS) firms and concentrated in the most productive firms.

**Relevance to this project:** Most relevant for our supply-chain/network perspective. Shows that carbon leakage operates through input sourcing by downstream firms, not just direct relocation. Their product-level classification of regulated goods and IO-based identification of regulated-intensive sectors is directly applicable.

**Notes/thoughts:** [Full notes](articles/notes_coster_digiovanni_mejean_2025.md)

---

### Mulier, Ovaere & Stimpfle (2024) — "Emission Trading and Overlapping Environmental Support"

**Key idea:** Studies interaction between EU ETS and national support policies using 24,000 state aid cases. Renewable energy support amplifies ETS effectiveness for power producers (+30% additional reduction), but cost compensation for energy-intensive manufacturing weakens it (-7% less reduction). The compensation undermines the carbon price signal.

**Relevance to this project:** NBB working paper — same institutional/data context as our Belgian setting. The finding that cost compensation weakens the carbon price signal is important for policy design. Uses the 2017 EU ETS regulatory tightening as identification.

**Notes/thoughts:** [Full notes](articles/notes_mulier_ovaere_stimpfle_2024.md)

---

### De Jonghe, Mulier & Schepens (2021) — "Climate Regulation, Firm Emissions, and Green Takeovers"

**Key idea:** After the 2017 EU ETS price increase, polluting firms improve emission efficiency and become more likely to acquire green firms within the ETS area. No evidence of carbon leakage through cross-border M&A. Free allowance allocation affects response strength. Precursor to Mulier et al. (2024) — same sample, identification, and data sources.

**Relevance to this project:** Green takeovers are a network phenomenon — polluting firms restructure by acquiring cleaner firms. The allowance shortage measure (verified emissions - free allowances / total assets) is a useful treatment intensity variable. Same EUTL + ORBIS + ZEPHYR data pipeline we could use.
